The CFPB complaint data and consumer reviews identify exactly which case types are failing to resolve. This document maps where each workflow breaks — and the four specific bets that would make a measurable difference right now.

The starting point
60% is solved. The problem lives in the other 40%.

Affirm's AI assistant in customer support chat already deflects over 60% of contacts without a human agent. That's a strong result — it means the simple, structured inquiries are largely handled. What's left in the 40% are the cases that require investigation, evidence gathering, or cross-system coordination. Those are exactly the cases generating CFPB complaints.

The key framing: The question isn't "should Affirm invest in AI for customer service?" — they already have. The question is where the AI boundary currently sits, and what specifically is failing in the cases that cross it. The CFPB data answers the second question precisely: credit errors, identity/fraud, and disputes are the dominant categories. This document is about the workflow inside those three.
Case workflow analysis
Where each high-volume case type breaks — and what the fix looks like

For each of the three dominant complaint categories from the CFPB data: one customer quote that illustrates the failure, the specific workflow friction point, and the targeted intervention. Workflow assumptions are noted explicitly.

Structured intake: In-app flow captures which bureau, what the error is, approximate date — before any agent. Case arrives pre-classified. Saves 2–3 min at intake

Identity & fraud — unauthorized account, unknown transaction, account compromised
22% of all filings

"My account has been locked for going on a couple of years now, with no help from multiple phone calls at various intervals. They keep telling me they can't verify my identity because the name doesn't match — even though my phone number, email, and date of birth are correct. I've expressed my account security concerns repeatedly and nothing moves."

Sitejabber · 1★ · Sep 2025 — pattern confirmed across 203 public reviews and 1,417 CFPB "information belongs to someone else" sub-issues
Assumption: Fraud cases are triaged manually after an agent creates the case. Investigation signals (device, location, velocity) exist in Affirm's system but are not automatically surfaced to analysts. Bureau remediation is handled separately from the fraud case itself.
⬜ Today
1
Customer reports fraud. Agent verifies identity, flags account. No case ID given. Customer has no evidence they were heard
2
Account frozen. Customer told "wait 30 days." No reference number. No portal to check status. Re-contact loop begins.
3
Fraud team investigates — device, IP, velocity. Each analyst may check different signals. No standardized checklist. Analyst-to-analyst variance in what gets reviewed.
4
Decision communicated. If denied: "we reviewed and confirmed this is your account." No evidence shared. No basis given. Customer files CFPB complaint.
5
If fraud confirmed: account closed. Bureau correction filed separately as a different case. Customer must call back again to fix credit report
✦ With the fix
1
Signal pre-score at case creation: Device match, geolocation anomaly, account-age, transaction velocity — scored automatically before analyst opens the case. Not a decision; a starting point. Analyst knows where to look before reading the complaint
2
Case ID + status portal immediately on creation: Customer receives a reference number and a link showing: evidence received → under review → decision made. No black box. Estimated 40–50% reduction in re-contact from status inquiries alone
3
Structured evidence intake: Customer prompted (via portal) for ID, police report if available, conflicting transaction evidence. Analyst receives an organized case file, not a free-form email thread. AI parses uploaded documents, pre-populates investigation fields
4
Standardized investigation checklist: Consistent signal review framework logged per case. Decision recorded with specific basis. Final fraud determination stays with a trained human analyst — always. AI assists, does not decide.
5
Decision with basis + bundled remediation: If fraud confirmed, bureau correction auto-triggered as part of the same case close — not a separate process. If denied, customer receives specific explanation of what signals were reviewed. Eliminates "won't explain the decision" CFPB trigger

The key insight: The biggest frustration in fraud cases is not the outcome — it's the opacity of the process. Customers who understand what was reviewed and why a decision was made are significantly less likely to escalate, even if the outcome went against them. A case portal with real-time status is a product/UX intervention that has faster ROI than changing the investigation model.

Customer receives specifics — and a clear explanation of how to submit additional evidence if they disagree. Agent makes the determination. AI reduces drafting time by ~3 min.

Case Architecture
How a credit error case flows end-to-end

Credit reporting errors are the highest-volume complex case type — 4,011 CFPB complaints, avg review rating 1.23 out of 5. The workflow below maps the full journey: from the moment a customer contacts Affirm to bureau confirmation and case closure. Two design decisions define this architecture: Metro 2 reconciliation is fully automated, and a Type A / Type B split separates deterministic fixes from cases that need agent judgment.

Legend AI / Automated Human agent Decision External / Bureau Resolution Channel / system
Metro 2 reconciliation is fully automated
No agent is needed to spot the discrepancy — the system compares what Affirm holds vs. what was last furnished to the bureau.
Type A / B handles judgment calls cleanly
Deterministic errors auto-submit. Ambiguous ones surface a pre-built diff context card so agent review takes minutes, not hours.
The loop doesn't close until confirmed
Bureau confirmation tracking turns a fire-and-forget submission into a closed system. Day 5 auto-escalate ensures nothing silently expires.
Where to start
Four specific bets — sequenced by effort vs. impact

Not everything described above needs to be built at once. These four bets are ordered by the ratio of impact to engineering effort. The first two don't require an AI model at all — they're workflow and communication problems. The latter two build on the data foundation the first two create.

Bet 1 — Do now · weeks, not months
Agent handover context card
When the AI bot escalates to a human, the agent opens a blank slate. The customer repeats their entire story. A structured handover — what the bot tried, what the customer said, pre-pulled account snapshot, case type prediction — is a presentation layer problem. Affirm already has all this data. It's just not surfaced at handover. Every complex case improves immediately.
No new data source Ships in weeks Target: -3–4 min AHT per case
Bet 2 — Do now · no AI required
Automated merchant SLA enforcement for disputes
Dispute cases have the worst satisfaction rating (1.09★). The root cause is simple: merchant response is slow and there's no enforcement. Day 0 auto-outreach → Day 5 reminder → Day 7 escalation with provisional credit consideration. This is a rule engine, not a model — and it removes the single biggest source of dispute case delay without waiting on engineering for an AI build.
Rule engine, not AI Ships in a sprint Addresses 1,721 cases/yr
Bet 3 — Next · medium complexity
Credit error status communication rebuild
4,011 CFPB cases. After a correction is submitted to a bureau, customers receive a generic form letter. Replace it with a specific notification: what was corrected, when it was submitted, confirmation when the bureau acknowledges receipt. The data for this exists — it's a trigger and template change on the existing notification system. Re-contact from "did anything actually change?" drops significantly.
Notification trigger + template Kills the re-contact inquiry Needs bureau confirmation loop first
Bet 4 — Medium-term · requires data foundation
CFPB escalation prediction model
The CFPB complaint pattern is learnable: dispute denied with no explanation + no acknowledgment of appeal path = CFPB filing within 30 days. A model that flags "this case has a high escalation probability before it closes" gives agents a window to proactively reach out with a better resolution. This requires the re-contact rate baseline and case history data that Bets 1–3 create — it's a Phase 2 build, not Day 1.
Requires Phase 1 data Target: -15% CFPB escalation rate ML model — higher build complexity

What to avoid: Three of the four bets are not AI problems — they're workflow and communication problems. The temptation in a PM role is to lead with the AI story because it sounds more ambitious. But the highest-ROI interventions here are a handover card, a scheduling rule, and a better notification template. Leading with those, then layering AI on top once the data foundation exists, is the more credible and more defensible sequence.
Success metrics
How to know if the bets are working

Each bet has a primary metric that makes its impact legible. Establishing baselines for all four before building anything is non-negotiable — without baselines, there is no way to demonstrate that anything shipped actually moved the needle.

Re-contact rate
% of cases that generate a second contact within 30 days on the same issue. Primary proxy for FCR. Baselining this by case type (credit, fraud, dispute) is the first action before any build starts.
Target: -30% · Bet 1 + 2
AHT by case type
Average handle time, segmented by credit error / fraud / dispute. Not aggregate AHT — aggregate hides the case types that are dragging the number. Need case-type-level visibility to measure Bet 1's handover card impact.
Target: -25% · Bet 1
Dispute resolution time
Days from dispute creation to resolution decision. Current baseline unknown — the merchant SLA enforcement (Bet 2) should compress this directly. Merchant response time is the primary sub-metric to instrument.
Target: -50% P75 · Bet 2
Credit correction cycle
Time from credit error case creation to confirmed bureau acknowledgment of correction. This metric doesn't exist today if there's no bureau confirmation tracking — Bet 3 creates the metric and moves it simultaneously.
Target: confirmed in <10 days · Bet 3
CFPB filing rate
CFPB complaints per 10K accounts, tracked monthly by case category. The lagging indicator — improvements in the metrics above should show up here 30–60 days later. Useful as the ultimate validation that the bets are producing real outcomes.
Target: -15% · Bet 4
